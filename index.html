Executive Brief

The $213 Billion Paradox

The world spends $213 billion a year on cybersecurity (Gartner 2025). Yet breaches remain at 3,000+ per year, 68% are caused by human error, and cybercrime damages have reached $10.5 trillion — 50x the defense budget. The problem was never a lack of tools.

The Numbers Don't Lie

Despite record spending, organizations with 50+ security tools are actually 8% worse at detecting threats than those with fewer tools (Ponemon). Large enterprises juggle 70-130 security products, yet 68% of breaches still stem from human error, social engineering, and misconfiguration — not from a gap in tooling (Verizon DBIR 2024). Meanwhile, 4.8 million cybersecurity positions remain unfilled globally (ISC2 2024), and the average breach still takes 241 days to detect.

  • $213B spent on security in 2025 — yet $10.5T in cybercrime damages (50x gap)
  • 3,158 data compromises in 2024 — 1.3B victim notices sent (211% increase)
  • 68% of breaches involve human error, not tool failure (Verizon DBIR)
  • Organizations with 50+ tools detect threats worse, not better (Ponemon)

What Actually Makes the Difference

It is not the most expensive security tools that protect an organization. It is cyber culture, business alignment, and leadership awareness that make all the difference. When security is treated as an engineering discipline aligned with business outcomes — rather than a checkbox procurement exercise — even a stack of open-source tools can deliver extraordinary results.

  • Cyber culture: security awareness embedded in every team, not just the SOC
  • Business alignment: every control maps to a real risk, not a vendor upsell
  • Leadership awareness: boards that understand risk, not just compliance status
  • Engineering mindset: security as code, transparent, auditable, AI-augmented

The real insight: Organizations do not get breached because they chose the wrong security product. They get breached because of misconfigured tools, untrained employees, poor incident response processes, and security programs disconnected from business reality. Phalanx Cyber exists to prove that a practitioner-built, open-source security platform — combined with the right cybersecurity mindset, engineering discipline, and business alignment — can deliver what matters most: real security outcomes. That is what organizations need in the AI era. Not more tools. Better thinking.

AI Security

Securing AI in the Enterprise

AI is transforming every business function — but it also introduces an entirely new attack surface. From prompt injection to model poisoning to data exfiltration through LLM outputs, organizations deploying AI need purpose-built security controls. Phalanx Cyber provides open-source AI security tooling across the full AI lifecycle.

The AI Threat Landscape

01

Prompt Injection

Attackers craft malicious inputs that override system prompts, bypass safety guardrails, or manipulate LLM behavior to extract sensitive data, execute unauthorized actions, or produce harmful outputs. Both direct and indirect injection vectors are actively exploited in production AI systems.

02

Data Poisoning & Training Risks

Compromised training datasets inject backdoors, biases, or malicious behaviors into models during the training phase. Poisoned models can produce subtly incorrect outputs, leak training data, or behave adversarially when triggered by specific inputs — often undetectable by standard testing.

03

Sensitive Data Exposure

LLMs can memorize and regurgitate PII, PHI, credentials, API keys, and proprietary information from training data. Without output filtering and data loss prevention controls, AI systems become unintentional data exfiltration channels — leaking sensitive information in seemingly innocuous responses.

04

Supply Chain Vulnerabilities

AI applications depend on a complex supply chain: pre-trained models from Hugging Face, pip/npm packages with known CVEs, ONNX/GGUF model files, and vector databases. A single compromised dependency — a poisoned model weight, a backdoored tokenizer — can undermine the entire AI stack.

05

Excessive Agency & Permissions

Agentic AI systems with tool-use capabilities (file access, code execution, API calls) can be manipulated into performing unauthorized actions. Overly permissive agent configurations, missing guardrails, and inadequate sandboxing turn AI assistants into attack vectors with system-level access.

06

Model Theft & Inference Attacks

Adversaries can extract proprietary models through systematic querying, replicate model behavior via distillation attacks, or perform membership inference to determine if specific data was used in training — creating IP theft, privacy, and competitive intelligence risks.
